在《紅樓夢》裡，賈元春的死，和前面那些女子都不一樣。 金釧兒是被一句羞辱逼死，晴雯是被一場誣陷趕出賈府，導致死亡，尤三姐是以死證明清白，尤二姐是被王熙鳳一點一點磨死。她們的死，多半發生在賈府內宅，發生在丫鬟、妾室、女子名節和家族規矩之中。可是賈元春的死，卻發生在更高、更冷、更黑暗的地方，那就是皇宮。 賈元春是賈寶玉的親姐姐，也是賈府真正的政治靠山。 她很早就入宮，先為女史，後來忽然「才選鳳藻宮」，被封為賢德妃。從那一刻起，賈府上下便像忽然被一道金光照亮。賈母、王夫人、賈政、賈赦，整個榮寧二府，都因為這位入宮的大小姐，一下子多了無限榮耀。 可是這榮耀，從一開始就帶著一股犀利的寒氣。 因為元春不是嫁人，她是被送進了宮。 那地方表面上是天下富貴至極之處，實際卻是天下最不自由的牢籠。後來元春省親，回到大觀園，見到父母祖母，竟不是歡歡喜喜，而是哭。 她一見賈母、王夫人，便忍不住流淚，說那皇宮是「不得見人的去處」。這一句話，很重，也很冷。它像一根針，輕輕刺破了賈府滿門榮耀的金紙。

寶玉和元春的感情，與旁人又不同。元春比寶玉年長許多，寶玉幼年時，她還在家中，曾親自教他讀書識字。她不像一般姐姐，而是長姊如母，更像半個母親。寶玉小時候在她膝前承歡，教他寫字，哄她玩耍，給他講故事，那種姊弟情分，是任何其他人不能比的。 所以元春省親那一夜，對寶玉來說，是極大的事。 大觀園裡燈火輝煌，張燈結綵，鼓樂齊鳴，亭臺樓閣，宛如仙境。賈府用盡財力，修出這樣一座人間富貴夢鄉，只為迎接元妃省親。可是那一夜，真正讓人難忘的，不是富貴，而是悲涼。 元春看見寶玉，心中自然喜愛。她命寶玉作詩，對他的才情格外欣賞。寶玉在她面前，既是弟弟，也是她心中無法割斷的家庭舊夢。她身在宮中，身不由己，回不來了，可寶玉還在家裡，還在那些姐妹中間，還能活在她失去的那片溫柔世界裡。 所以元春對寶玉，不僅是疼愛，更是把他看成對家族前途的寄託。 她希望寶玉好，希望他讀書成才，希望他將來能撐住賈府門楣。可是寶玉偏偏不是那樣的人，他厭惡仕途經濟，厭惡功名利祿。他愛的是大觀園裡那些女孩，愛的是詩詞、花草、真情、眼淚。元春也許懂他一些，但她身在宮中，早已不可能由著自己的真心活著。 這就是元春的悲劇。 她本來也是賈府的女兒，也應該有自己的青春、愛情與人生。可她一入宮，便成了家族的門面，成了政治的籌碼，成了賈府富貴的根基。她活著，賈府便有靠山；她一死，賈府的天便塌了一半。 通行本裡，元春死在第九十五回。書中說她身體發福，舉動費力，後因痰疾發作，病入膏肓而亡。這樣寫，表面上很平穩，好像不過是一場疾病而已。 可是《紅樓夢》前八十回裡， 曹雪芹早已埋下更冷的伏筆。 第五回中，元春的判詞寫道：「榴花開處照宮闈，虎兕相逢大夢歸。」 又有曲子《恨無常》說： 「喜榮華正好，恨無常又到。眼睜睜，把萬事全拋。蕩悠悠，把芳魂消耗。望家鄉，路遠山遙。」 這些話，怎麼看，元春都不像是普通病死。 「虎兕相逢大夢歸」，帶著宮廷爭鬥的殺氣。「蕩悠悠，把芳魂消耗」，更不像安然離世。她的魂不是平靜歸去，而像被什麼力量拖著、耗著、折磨著，最後消散在深宮高牆之內。 更可怕的是，有些紅學家認為，元春原本的結局，很可能不是病死，而是在宮廷鬥爭中失勢，被賜死，甚至被縊死。第五回判詞畫中「一張弓，弓上掛著香櫞」，也常被解讀為暗示弓弦勒命。若真如此，元春之死，就不是一個貴妃病逝，而是一個女子被皇權吞噬。 她曾經是賈府最大的榮耀，最後卻可能死在那個賈府無人能進、無人能救、無人敢問的地方。 寶玉聽到元春死訊時，心中的震動，恐怕不是一般人能理解的。 那是他的親姐姐，是幼年教過他讀書寫字的人，是曾經在省親夜裡含淚看著他的人，她是整個賈府最尊貴、也最孤獨的女子。 她的死，不只是寶玉親人的死，更是寶玉對富貴世界的一次大幻滅。原來賈府拼命攀附的那座皇宮，並不能給人幸福。原來所謂封妃，所謂榮耀，所謂光宗耀祖，最後也不過是一場「大夢歸」。 元春死後，賈府失去了最重要的靠山。從此之後，敗落便再也遮不住了。那些原來還能維持的體面，那些靠權勢支撐的富貴，那些人前人後的風光，都開始一層一層塌下來。 寶玉也從元春之死中，看見了另一種女子的命運。 像晴雯那樣的丫鬟會死，像金釧兒那樣的丫鬟會死，像尤二姐、尤三姐那樣被捲進賈府漩渦的女子會死。可是到了元春這裡，寶玉終於明白，原來就算做了貴妃，到了天下最尊貴的地方，也一樣逃不過死。 甚至死得更孤獨，更不能說，更沒有真相。 元春這一生，像一朵石榴花，開在宮牆深處，紅得耀眼，卻無人能真正靠近。她照亮過賈府的門楣，也燃盡了自己的命。 她的死，讓寶玉更深地看清了這個世界。 富貴不真； 榮耀不真； 皇恩不真； 家族拿女兒換來的榮華，也不真； 最後，只剩下一個「恨無常」，深宮歸天的賈元春。 從這一天起，寶玉心裡那座大觀園，又塌了一角。 （未完待續）
Among all the women who die in Dream of the Red Chamber, Jia Yuanchun's death is unlike any of the others. Jinchuan was driven to death by a single humiliation. Qingwen was falsely accused, expelled from the Jia Mansion, and ultimately died. You Sanjie chose death to preserve her honor. You Erjie was slowly destroyed by Wang Xifeng, little by little. Most of these deaths took place within the inner quarters of the Jia household, among maids, concubines, women's reputations, and the rigid rules of the family. Yuanchun's death, however, occurred in a place far higher, colder, and darker—the Imperial Palace. Jia Yuanchun was Jia Baoyu's elder sister and the true political pillar of the Jia family. She entered the palace at an early age, first serving as a Lady-in-Waiting. Later, she was suddenly selected for Fengzao Palace and granted the title of Worthy Consort. From that moment on, it seemed as though a golden light had suddenly descended upon the Jia family. Grandmother Jia, Lady Wang, Jia Zheng, Jia She—indeed, both the Rong and Ning Mansions—all found themselves bathed in unprecedented glory because of this eldest daughter who had entered the palace. She entered the palace at an early age, first serving as a Lady-in-Waiting. Later, she was suddenly selected for Fengzao Palace and granted the title of Worthy Consort. From that moment on, it seemed as though a golden light had suddenly descended upon the Jia family. Grandmother Jia, Lady Wang, Jia Zheng, Jia She—indeed, both the Rong and Ning Mansions—all found themselves bathed in unprecedented glory because of this eldest daughter who had entered the palace. Yet from the very beginning, that glory carried with it a piercing chill. For Yuanchun had not married，She had been sent into the palace. Outwardly, it was the most magnificent place of wealth and honor under Heaven. In reality, it was the least free prison in the world. Later, when Yuanchun was permitted to visit her family and returned to the Grand View Garden, she did not greet her parents and grandmother with joy. Instead, she wept. The moment she saw Grandmother Jia and Lady Wang, tears filled her eyes. She said that the Imperial Palace was "a place where one cannot truly see another human being." Those words were heavy, cold, and unforgettable. Like a needle, they quietly pierced the golden paper that covered the Jia family's glorious reputation. Others saw only that Yuanchun had become an Imperial Consort and that the Jia family had risen to power. Only Yuanchun herself knew what life inside the palace was truly like. Baoyu's bond with Yuanchun was unlike anyone else's. She was many years older than he was. When Baoyu was still a child, she was still living at home and had personally taught him to read and write. She was more than an elder sister. She was almost like a second mother. As a little boy, Baoyu had sat happily beside her, learning his characters, listening to her stories, and hearing her gentle voice. No one else could ever replace that bond. So on the night of Imperial Consort Yuanchun's homecoming, it meant everything to Baoyu. The Grand View Garden blazed with brilliant lights. Music filled the air. Pavilions and terraces stood adorned with elegant inscriptions. The Jia family spared no expense in creating this earthly dream of wealth and splendor, all for the sake of welcoming Imperial Consort Yuanchun home. Yet what people remembered most from that night was not its magnificence, but its sorrow. Yuanchun was naturally delighted to see Baoyu again. She asked him to compose poetry and showed particular admiration for his literary talent. Before her, Baoyu was not only her younger brother, but also the last living reminder of the family life she had lost forever. She herself could never return from the palace, yet Baoyu still remained at home, still lived among his sisters, and still inhabited the gentle world that had long since slipped from her own hands. That was why Yuanchun's affection for Baoyu was more than sisterly love. She also placed in him the hopes of the entire Jia family. She wanted him to succeed. She hoped he would devote himself to study, achieve official distinction, and one day uphold the honor of the Jia family. But Baoyu was never that kind of person. He despised the pursuit of official rank and worldly success. What he loved were the girls of the Grand View Garden, poetry, flowers, genuine feeling, and tears. Perhaps Yuanchun understood him better than most. But living within the palace, she had long ago lost the freedom to live according to her own heart. That was the true tragedy of Jia Yuanchun. She had once been simply another daughter of the Jia family, entitled to her own youth, her own love, and her own life. Yet the moment she entered the palace, she became the family's public face, a political bargaining piece, and the very foundation of the Jia family's prosperity. As long as she lived, the Jia family possessed a powerful protector. The day she died, half the sky above the Jia family collapsed. In the commonly circulated 120-chapter edition of Dream of the Red Chamber, Yuanchun dies in Chapter Ninety-Five. The novel describes her as having grown heavy in body, moving with difficulty, and eventually dying after a severe illness brought on by a respiratory disorder. On the surface, it appears to be nothing more than an illness within the palace. Yet the first eighty chapters of Dream of the Red Chamber had already planted far darker hints. In Chapter Five, Yuanchun's prophetic verse reads: Where pomegranate blossoms illuminate the palace courts, When tiger and rhinoceros meet, the great dream returns. Another song, "The Hatred of Impermanence," declares: Just when glory reaches its height, Impermanence suddenly arrives. Helplessly, all things are abandoned. Slowly drifting away, The fragrant soul is worn away. Looking toward home, The road is distant, the mountains far away. Read together, these verses hardly suggest an ordinary death from illness. "The tiger and the rhinoceros meet, and the great dream returns" carries the ominous scent of deadly palace intrigue. "The fragrant soul is slowly worn away" sounds even less like a peaceful passing. Her soul does not quietly depart. It seems to be dragged away, exhausted, tormented, and finally extinguished behind the towering walls of the Imperial Palace. More unsettling still, some scholars of Redology believe that Yuanchun's original fate was never meant to be death by illness at all. They suggest that she fell from favor during a struggle within the Imperial Court, was ordered to die by imperial decree, or was even executed by strangulation. In the illustration accompanying her prophetic verse in Chapter Five appears "a bow with a fragrant citron hanging from its string." Many scholars interpret this image as a symbol of death by a bowstring tightened around the neck. If that interpretation is correct, then Yuanchun did not simply die as an Imperial Consort. She became yet another woman devoured by imperial power. She had once been the greatest source of glory for the Jia family. In the end, however, she may have died in the one place where no member of the Jia family could enter, no one could rescue her, and no one even dared ask what had truly happened. When Baoyu learned of Yuanchun's death, the shock he felt was something few people could truly understand. She was his own elder sister. She was the one who had taught him to read and write when he was a child. She was the one who had looked at him with tears in her eyes on the night of her homecoming. She was both the most honored and the loneliest woman in the entire Jia family. Her death was not merely the loss of someone dear to Baoyu. It was also the collapse of one of his greatest illusions about the world of wealth and privilege. He finally realized that the Imperial Palace，the place upon which the Jia family had pinned all its hopes，could not bring happiness. The title of Imperial Consort... The glory... The honor of bringing distinction to one's ancestors... In the end, they were all nothing more than "the return of a great dream." After Yuanchun's death, the Jia family lost its greatest protector. From that moment onward, its decline could no longer be concealed. The dignity it had struggled to preserve... The wealth sustained by political influence... The splendor displayed before the world... All of it began, layer by layer, to collapse. Through Yuanchun's death, Baoyu also came to understand yet another kind of female destiny. Maids like Qingwen could die. Maids like Jinchuan could die. Women like You Erjie and You Sanjie, swept into the whirlpool of the Jia family, could die. But with Yuanchun, Baoyu finally understood something even more terrible: Even a woman who became an Imperial Consort...Even a woman who reached the highest and most honored place beneath Heaven...Could not escape death. Indeed, she might die even more alone, with even less freedom to speak, and with the truth buried forever. Yuanchun's life was like a pomegranate blossom blooming deep within the palace walls，brilliantly red, dazzling to behold, yet forever beyond anyone's reach. She illuminated the honor of the Jia family，she also burned away her own life. Her death allowed Baoyu to see this world more clearly than ever before. Wealth is an illusion; Glory is an illusion; Imperial favor is an illusion; Even the prosperity a family gains by sacrificing its daughters is an illusion. In the end, all that remains is "The Hatred of Impermanence", and Jia Yuanchun, who returned to Heaven from behind the palace walls. From that day forward, another corner of the Grand View Garden collapsed within Baoyu's heart. (To be continued)
