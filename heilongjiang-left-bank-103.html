青兒和盧正鋼走了整整一個上午，腳上的棉鞋早已被雪水浸透，褲腿上也結了一層冰。 翻過一道山梁後，青兒忽然停下腳步，伸手指向遠方。 「到了。」 盧正鋼順著她手指的方向望去，只見山腳下的樹林邊，隱隱升起一股青灰色的炊煙。被風吹得在屋子上空飄散，好似一幅潑墨水彩畫，在白雪覆蓋的世界裡顯得格外醒目。 兩人精神一振，加快了腳步。 又走了半個多時辰，一座低矮的木屋終於出現在眼前。 木屋很舊，全部松樹原木搭建，屋頂壓著厚厚的積雪。院子裡堆放著像小山一樣，劈好的木柴，外面的曬衣繩上， 掛著幾條已經凍硬的大馬哈魚。 青兒剛走到門口，就聽到屋裡傳出一陣劇烈的咳嗽聲。 緊接著，房間門被推開。 一位頭髮花白的老婦人慢慢走了出來。

「聽老張頭傳話， 說你們要調查當年老毛子禍害咱們中國人的那些事？ 這麼多年過去了，我現在想起來都頭皮發麻， 現在誰還想那些傷心事呀？」 青兒說：「 王奶奶， 當年老毛子害死咱們那麼多人， 這筆帳還沒跟他們算呢， 可是現在就有人希望我們把這些事忘掉。 我們調查這些事， 就是不想讓那麼多人的鮮血白流。 我們要把這些事， 告訴全中國人民， 告訴全世界人民， 讓大家都知道， 老毛子，當年的俄國人， Qing'er and Lu Zhenggang had been walking the entire morning. Their cotton shoes had long since been soaked through by melting snow, and a thin layer of ice had formed around the bottoms of their trouser legs. After crossing a mountain ridge, Qing'er suddenly stopped and pointed into the distance. "We're here." Lu Zhenggang followed the direction of her finger. At the edge of a grove beneath the mountain, a thin plume of bluish-gray smoke rose quietly into the air. Blown by the wind, it drifted above a lonely cabin like the brushstrokes of an ink-and-watercolor painting, standing out vividly against the endless white landscape. Their spirits lifted, and they quickened their pace. After walking for another half hour or so, a low wooden cabin finally came into view. A mountain of neatly split firewood stood in the yard, while several frozen chum salmon hung stiffly from the clothesline outside. She was obviously well advanced in years. Though thin and frail, she stood remarkably straight. She wore an old fox-fur vest whose fur had nearly been worn away with age. A cloth sash was tied around her waist, and in her hand she carried a polished Manchurian brass-tipped tobacco pipe nearly three feet long. The moment she saw Qing'er, her face broke into a radiant smile. "Oh! You must be Qing'er! Old Zhang sent word ahead. I've been waiting for you these past few days. What an honest, silly child you are—you didn't even wait for better weather. Such a heavy snow! You must be exhausted. Come inside quickly and warm up yourselves . Speaking briskly in her straightforward manner, the old woman ushered the two of them into the house. Qing'er smiled and answered softly before introducing the young man beside her. "Grandma Wang, this is Lu Zhenggang." The old woman studied him for nearly a full minute before finally nodding with satisfaction. "What a fine young man. Qing'er, you've got good taste." Qing'er's face immediately flushed. "Grandma Wang, what are you talking about? Please don't misunderstand. We're just friends. We've come together only to investigate history." Grandma Wang burst into hearty laughter. "Oh, Grandma isn't misunderstanding anything. But if you ask me, you two make a perfect pair. You're friends now—but who knows what the future may bring?" Qing'er glanced at Lu Zhenggang. He simply smiled without saying a word. Qing'er's cheeks grew even redder. Grandma Wang was an open-hearted, straightforward woman whose voice was still full of strength. Had one only heard her speak, no one would have believed she was already well past seventy. In her booming voice she said, "I'm so happy you've come. With snow this heavy, don't think about leaving today. Stay here for a few days. Grandma will make sauerkraut dumplings stuffed with roe deer meat for you." Qing'er and Lu Zhenggang looked around the house. From the outside it appeared rather small, but inside it consisted of three rooms. The kitchen occupied the middle, with living quarters on both the east and west sides. Grandma Wang led them into the room on the east side, which was evidently her bedroom. It contained one large heated kang that stretched across the room. The first thing that caught the eye was an old hunting rifle hanging on the wall. Though aged, it had been polished until it was spotless. It was obvious that its owner treasured it deeply and had taken excellent care of it. The furnishings were extremely simple: a wooden table, two long benches, and an old kang cabinet. "Grandma Wang," Qing'er asked after noticing the rifle, "do you still go into the mountains to hunt?" "Not very often anymore," Grandma Wang replied. "I'm getting old, and these legs don't carry me the way they used to. Before winter set in, I went into the mountains and brought back a few hares, some pheasants, and a roe deer. That'll be enough meat for the whole winter. Today I'll make sauerkraut dumplings filled with roe deer meat for you." Grandma Wang first poured each of them a bowl of hot water. "I don't have anything fancy to offer my guests..." she said with an apologetic smile. Then she brought out a small basket of hazelnuts and invited them to help themselves. She then stepped out to the middle kitchen and added several pine logs to the wood-burning stove. The stove was connected to the heated kangs in both side rooms. Once the fire was burning, the kangs gradually warmed, and soon the entire house became comfortably warm. Lu Zhenggang placed his backpack on the table and took out two bags of fruit candies that he had bought at the supply cooperative in the village below the mountain. Handing them to Grandma Wang, he said, "There isn't much to buy up here in the mountains. Please accept these fruit candies—they may help brighten your day." The old woman smiled the moment she saw the candies. "Look at you two honest children," she said. "Why be so polite? You even brought me gifts. I'm just an old widow, not a little child anymore. What would I do with candy? Take it back and enjoy it yourselves." Smiling, Qing'er accepted the candies Grandma Wang tried to return and quietly placed them inside the kang cabinet for her. "Please keep them, Grandma. Winter is cold. A little candy helps keep you warm and gives you energy." Grandma Wang nodded happily and no longer insisted. After everyone had settled down, Grandma Wang asked, "Old Zhang sent word that you've come to investigate what those Russian devils did to our Chinese people all those years ago. It's been decades now, yet every time I think about it, my scalp still tingles. Who still wants to remember such heartbreaking things?" Qing'er answered, "Grandma Wang, those Russian invaders killed so many of our people, and that debt has never been settled. Yet now there are people who hope we will simply forget what happened. We are investigating these events because we refuse to let the blood of so many innocent people have been shed in vain. We want to tell all the people of China—and people throughout the world—what really happened, so everyone will know how cruel those Russian invaders were. As long as we deal with them, we must always remain vigilant." Grandma Wang remained silent for a long time after hearing Qing'er's words. It was obvious that old memories had stirred deep sorrow within her. After quite a while, she finally spoke. "Those things happened decades ago. People hardly mention them anymore. Yet the two of you came all this way through such heavy snow just for this. Then it must truly be something important. Go ahead and ask whatever you want. I'll tell you everything I know. You're right. The blood of our Chinese people must not have been shed in vain. Everyone should know what those damned Russian devils did to us in those days." Grandma Wang packed the bowl of her long tobacco pipe with homegrown tobacco, struck a spark with her flint, lit it, and took a deep draw. A thin ribbon of pale blue smoke slowly curled upward and drifted through the room. The old woman narrowed her eyes, lost in thought. Her face grew solemn. After sitting quietly for a long moment, she finally began to speak. "Old Zhang told me you wanted to know how I escaped from the hands of those Russian devils. Then I'll begin at the beginning. I was only sixteen when it happened. Back then I was considered one of the prettiest girls for dozens of miles around. Now..." She smiled bitterly. "Now I'm just an old woman beyond recognition." Qing'er immediately replied with a smile, "You're still beautiful, Grandma Wang. You're still a great beauty." The old woman burst into laughter. She laughed so hard that her whole body trembled and she bent forward at the waist. "You really know how to make an old woman happy," she said. "But I do love hearing that." The old woman continued, "Back then our family lived in Boldodo Village, on the left bank of the Heilongjiang River. It was a lively place in those days. People farmed, fished, brewed wine, ran shops, and opened restaurants. It was truly a land blessed by Heaven." She slowly stretched out her thin, weathered hand and gestured through the air. "It was a beautiful place in those days. In summer, if you stood on the riverbank, you could see fields stretching farther than the eye could reach. Soybeans, wheat, sorghum, and corn swayed in the breeze like waves upon the sea. Back then, everyone knew they were living in the Great Qing Empire. Boldodo Village lay within Qing territory. Generation after generation had lived there. It was our home." At that point, the old woman fell silent. Outside, the cold winter wind swept across the roof, carrying with it a long, mournful howl. The smile slowly faded from Grandma Wang's face. She drew deeply on her pipe, remained silent for a long while, and then slowly said, "Then... the Russian soldiers came." The atmosphere inside the room suddenly grew heavy. Qing'er quietly opened her notebook, while Lu Zhenggang picked up his pencil, ready to record every word. They exchanged a glance, but neither of them spoke. After another long pause, the old woman began again in a low, measured voice. "It was the twenty-sixth year of Emperor Guangxu's reign—the Gengzi Year, 1900. I was sixteen years old then. I was already engaged, and in another two months I was supposed to be married." "But none of us ever imagined that the Russian soldiers would suddenly storm into our village. They looked like demons. The moment they entered, they asked no questions and showed no mercy. They looted everything they could lay their hands on. They killed anyone they saw—some were shot, others were run through with bayonets. Then they set the houses on fire, turning a peaceful village into a living hell." "I was standing outside our house when my father realized something terrible was happening and pulled me back inside. We hadn't even had time to decide where to hide before the Russian soldiers burst through our door. I saw everything with my own eyes. A tall Russian soldier raised his rifle and shot my father to the ground without a word. Another, shorter soldier noticed that I was young and pretty. Thinking I would be easy prey, he suddenly had evil intentions." He shouted in Russian, 'Эта маленькая девочка — красивая...' "In Chinese, that means, 'The little girl... she's beautiful.'" Then he laid down his rifle and reached out to grab me. "I saw at once what he intended, and there was no way I was going to let him catch me. I twisted away from his outstretched hand and ran for the door. Ever since I was little, I had followed my father and grandfather deep into the mountains, hunting through the forests. Those years had made me swift and agile. I dashed out of the yard and ran straight toward the mountains. The Russian soldier must have flown into a rage. By the time he snatched up his rifle and tried to shoot me, I had already disappeared from sight." I escaped and saved my life. But nearly everyone in the village—men and women, the old and the young, more than a thousand people—was later killed by the Russian soldiers. Almost every house was burned to the ground, and everything of value, including the livestock, was looted. That night, unable to stop worrying about my family, I secretly returned to the village. The horrors I saw there are something I will never forget for as long as I live. The Russian soldiers seemed determined to wipe out every person in Boldodo Village. When I returned, corpses lay everywhere along the streets—men, women, the elderly, and children. In that large village that had once been home to more than a thousand people, I did not see a single person still alive... When I returned, the entire village held nothing but dead bodies and the charred ruins of collapsed houses. Only later did I learn that the Russian soldiers had found it too troublesome to shoot everyone one by one. Those they had not yet had time to kill—men and women, the old and the young alike—were driven toward the Heilongjiang River. Anyone who refused to move was shot or stabbed to death with a bayonet. At the riverbank, they forced everyone into the water and left them to drown. Those who knew how to swim and tried to escape were shot in the river. Our village had more than a thousand people, but fewer than ten survived. I was one of those who escaped death only by sheer fortune. When Grandma Wang reached this point, she suddenly stopped. She let out a long, heavy breath, and her eyes turned red. She tapped the bowl of her tobacco pipe against the sole of her shoe. Tears began to flow silently down her face. (To be Continued)
