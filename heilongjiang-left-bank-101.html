那是冬天的第一場大雪，夜裡就下起來。 寒風從北方吹來，穿過一望無際的黑松林，發出低沉而悠長的呼嘯聲，宛如遠古野獸在森林深處發出的吼叫。 木屋孤零零地立在山坡上，四周除了森林，還是森林。離這裡最近的村莊，也在三十多里之外。 此刻，厚厚的積雪已經覆蓋了屋頂。屋簷下垂掛著一排長長的冰凌，在月光下閃著寒冷的銀光。 屋子中間有一個一尺多深的火坑， 裡面交叉堆著粗粗的松木， 紅紅的火焰，使這個獵人留下的小木屋， 充滿暖意。 火焰跳動著，發出輕微的噼啪聲。 青兒蹲在火堆旁，正用一把獵刀，削著一根松木棍，好像是什麼事讓她分了心。 她的手停住不動，神情若有所思，美麗， 嚴肅。 她穿著厚厚的羊皮襖，頭髮用一條藍布帶，在腦後扎成一個小馬尾。火光映照著她的臉龐，使她原本白皙的皮膚透出一層淡淡的紅暈。

The first heavy snowfall of winter began during the night. A bitter wind swept down from the north, passing through the endless black pine forest. It let out a deep, lingering howl, like the roar of some ancient beast hidden in the heart of the wilderness. A lonely log cabin stood on the hillside. Beyond it there was nothing but forest in every direction. The nearest village lay more than thirty li away. By now, a thick blanket of snow had covered the roof. Long icicles hung from the eaves, glistening with a cold silver light beneath the moon. In the middle of the cabin was a fire pit more than a foot deep, where thick pine logs had been stacked in a crisscross pattern. Bright red flames filled the little hunting cabin with warmth. The fire crackled softly as the flames danced. Qing'er crouched beside the fire, carving a pine branch with a hunting knife. Something seemed to have distracted her. Her hand suddenly stopped moving. Lost in thought, she wore an expression that was both beautiful and solemn. She was dressed in a thick sheepskin coat. Her hair was tied into a short ponytail with a strip of blue cloth. The firelight illuminated her face, casting a gentle rosy glow upon her naturally fair skin.

The fire crackled softly as the flames danced. Qing'er crouched beside the fire, carving a pine branch with a hunting knife. Something seemed to have distracted her. Her hand suddenly stopped moving. Lost in thought, she wore an expression that was both beautiful and solemn. She was dressed in a thick sheepskin coat. Her hair was tied into a short ponytail with a strip of blue cloth. The firelight illuminated her face, casting a gentle rosy glow upon her naturally fair skin. No one would have imagined that this young woman, now living in an abandoned hunter's cabin deep in the mountains and surviving by hunting and gathering wild berries, had been a student in the Department of History at Peking University only three years earlier. Still less would anyone have imagined that only three years before, she had been a starting player on Peking University's women's basketball team, racing across the court beneath countless eyes filled with admiration, affection, and, inevitably, envy. Across the fire, Lu Zhenggang sat bent over a rough but sturdy table, writing. The table had been built by the two of them from birch planks with their own hands. A kerosene lamp stood on the table, casting a dim yellow glow over an open notebook whose pages were densely covered with writing. Some entries were names of people. Others were the names of villages. Still others were dates. July, 1900. Hailanpao. The Sixty-Four Villages East of the River. … Lu Zhenggang finally laid down his pen, glanced toward the absent-minded Qing'er, and rubbed his tired, aching eyes. "Are you still thinking about what the old villagers told us today?" Qing'er did not raise her head. She merely murmured a quiet, "Mm." Lu Zhenggang nodded. "What Grandpa Zhang told us today... I'd never heard that part before." Qing'er laid down the carving knife and gazed into the fire. The flames flickered softly in her eyes. "Which part?" she asked quietly. "The part about the young girl trying to escape?" Lu Zhenggang fell silent for a moment before speaking slowly. "He said the riverbank was filled with the sound of people crying. Russian soldiers drove the civilians toward the river. Some mothers knelt on the ground, clutching their children and begging for mercy, but the soldiers stabbed them to death with their bayonets. Others had barely leaped into the river before Russian bullets cut them down. The river was covered with floating bodies. The blood of the victims turned the water red." The cabin suddenly fell silent. Only the crackling of the burning pine logs could be heard. Qing'er lowered her head without saying a word. She had heard these stories ever since she was a child，far too many times to count. Yet every time she heard them again, her heart still ached. Among those who had perished were her own ancestors. Some were her grandfather's relatives. Countless others had not even left their names behind. Outside, the wind suddenly grew stronger, causing the wooden door to tremble gently. Lu Zhenggang stood up and checked the door bar once more. When he turned back, he saw Qing'er gazing absently at an old map hanging on the wall. It was a hand-drawn map that had long since yellowed with age. The edges were worn and frayed, and many place names had been carefully marked upon it. Hailanpao. Aigun. Bordo Village. The Mouth of the Jingqili River. One location, however, had been heavily circled in red ink： The Sixty-Four Villages East of the River. Lu Zhenggang knew that this map was Qing'er's most treasured possession. It was also one of the principal reasons，and one of the pieces of evidence，used to brand her a Rightist. Because this was the history she had chosen to study. It was a history that many people preferred never to mention.And a history that others deliberately wished to erase. Suddenly Qing'er spoke. "Zhenggang." "Yes?" "Do you regret it?" Lu Zhenggang looked up, slightly puzzled. "Regret what?" "Leaving university ⋯⋯ ，and following me here to live like wilderness people." A log suddenly burst apart in the fire, sending several sparks dancing into the air. Lu Zhenggang smiled. It was a faint smile, but a sincere one. "If I regretted it, I never would have come." Qing'er looked at him for a long time without speaking. She knew： For her sake, the man before her had given up Beijing, given up the university, and given up everything that might once have belonged to him. Thinking of this, she suddenly felt her nose sting. She lowered her head and pretended to continue carving the wooden stick. No one spoke again. The firelight quietly flickered, illuminating the cabin, the notebook upon the table, and the yellowed map upon the wall. No one knew that, only a few dozen li away at the foot of the mountain, people had already begun paying attention to them. Some were asking: "Those two Rightists who were condemned during the Anti-Rightist Campaign have hidden themselves deep in the mountains. Every day they visit old villagers and record stories from the past. What exactly are they trying to do?" Nor did anyone know that another disaster was already drawing, little by little, toward the lonely cabin. (To be continued)
