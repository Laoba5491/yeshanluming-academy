In western England, near the bustling city of Birmingham, there lies a quiet town known for breeding elegant British Shorthair cats. In this town lived an eight-year-old boy named Viktor. He had bright eyes and a head of messy brown hair. Viktor was lively and energetic, yet often felt lonely. His parents and two older sisters were always occupied with work and studies, leaving little time for him. To gain their attention, Viktor began staging small dramas. On the grass outside, he would pretend to be injured, crying out loudly for help. Each time, his family would rush out in alarm, tending to him with concern. At first, when they discovered it was a trick, they found it amusing—even clever. But as the performances repeated again and again, their patience wore thin. Eventually, they stopped responding. Viktor felt hurt and abandoned. Yet he continued the act. Because no one cared. One gloomy autumn day, real danger arrived. A large black wolf emerged from the distant forest, starving, searching for food. By chance, it noticed Viktor playing alone near the edge of the village. As the wolf approached, Viktor screamed in terror. But this time, no one came. They thought it was just another of his tricks. Facing the ferocious animal alone, Viktor’s fear turned into desperate resistance. With his small hands, he fought against the wolf with all his strength. But he was too weak. The wolf seized his leg and began dragging him toward the forest. At that critical moment, Ranger Sam—an experienced woodsman and hunter—heard Viktor’s cries. He rushed over and saw the wolf dragging the boy away. Without hesitation, he raised his rifle and fired. The bullet struck the wolf through the head, saving Viktor’s life. Viktor survived. But he lost a leg. Doctors did everything they could, but there was no way to save it. Later, he was fitted with a prosthetic limb. From that painful lesson, Viktor did not lose his courage. He trained hard, rebuilding his strength. In time, he won a gold medal in the 100-meter swimming event at the Paralympic Games—a world champion. After graduating from university, Viktor returned to his hometown and became a primary school teacher. He often told his students a story. “In a distant and ancient land called China,” he would begin, “there was a little boy named Wang Erxiao. Every day, he went up the mountain to tend sheep. He was a good child—except for one thing. He liked to lie, to trick others, to gain attention. Because he was lonely.” “One day, he shouted: ‘The wolf is coming! The wolf is coming!’” Farmers working nearby—including his parents—dropped their tools and ran to help him. But it was a lie. Wang Erxiao laughed, and the villagers left in frustration. The next day, he did it again. This time, most people did not come. Only his parents, unable to ignore their son, rushed over—only to be deceived once more. People mocked them. “Look at those parents—so foolish.” Day after day, the boy repeated the same trick. Day after day, his parents continued to respond—willing to be deceived, simply out of love. Until one day, even they grew tired. They stopped coming. No one expected that on the final day, it would be real. The wolf truly came. No one responded—not even his parents. And the poor boy, Wang Erxiao, was devoured. His parents were filled with regret…” The children listened, deeply moved. They believed in the teacher’s conclusion: “To be a good person, one must be honest. Never deceive others—or you may harm both others and yourself.” Some of the girls even cried, feeling sorrow for the distant boy in that faraway land. Viktor never told them what had happened to him. He never showed them his prosthetic leg. The children never suspected. They never imagined that their teacher—the one they admired so much—was, in truth, that same boy in the story. Nor did they ever think that their teacher had only one real leg. After all， He walked faster than anyone else. How could he possibly have a prosthetic limb? （ The end )
