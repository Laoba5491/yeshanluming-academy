It was the summer of 1966 in Beijing. Under a blazing sun, the ancient cultural capital sweltered beneath an unbearable heat. The air itself seemed heavy enough to suffocate. Yet hotter still than the weather was the political frenzy engulfing the entire city. Red flags fluttered everywhere. War drums thundered. Slogans such as "To Rebel Is Justified!" and "If the enemy refuses to surrender, destroy him!" echoed through every street and alley of Beijing. Having been personally received by Mao Zedong and swept away by revolutionary fervor, the young Red Guards marched proudly through the city. Dressed in olive-green military uniforms, broad brass-buckled belts fastened around their waists, and red armbands wrapped around their sleeves, they were impossible to overlook. They roamed the streets in every direction, eyes wide open, searching every corner of the city for anything that still bore the slightest trace of what they condemned as feudalism, capitalism, or revisionism. On the streets they cut open people's wide-legged trousers, chopped the pointed toes from leather shoes, and smashed the shop signs bearing the calligraphy of famous historical figures such as the Qianlong Emperor, Qi Gong, and Guo Moruo. But even that was no longer enough to satisfy the revolutionary passion raging inside them. The revolution had to continue. The revolution had to go deeper. Through action they would prove their devotion to the Great Leader. They were willing to stake even their own lives to defend his Great Proletarian Cultural Revolution and its revolutionary line. To carry the revolution one step further meant more than denouncing everything associated with feudalism, capitalism, and revisionism. It meant driving every remnant of the old feudal order out of Beijing—and, if necessary, wiping them out physically. How could the capital where the Great Leader lived be allowed to shelter relics of the old dynasty? Beijing was the sacred city of the revolution, a place that should be as pure and transparent as crystal, a revolutionary sanctuary where only the proletariat deserved to live. The weather burned with heat. Revolutionary passion burned even hotter. And the actions of the young Red Guards burned with madness. Human life had become worthless. Law had ceased to exist. The Red Guards possessed absolute power. By their own judgment alone, they could decide whether a person lived or died. They could pronounce a death sentence on the spot—and carry it out immediately. The brass-buckled belts fastened around their waists became instruments of justice as they understood it: simple, swift, convenient, and brutally efficient. No one respected the law any longer. The old order had completely collapsed. Freed from every legal restraint, the Red Guards were writing, through their own frenzy, one of the most chaotic, violent, and absurd chapters in the history of the People's Republic. The Cultural Revolution was remaking this ancient city with overwhelming force—rewriting its past, reshaping its present, and deciding its future. In Dongzongbu Alley, in Beijing's Dongcheng District, stood a secluded courtyard hidden from public view. Its gate remained closed almost all the time, and the owner of the house rarely appeared in public. Its secrecy was no different from that of countless other courtyards concealed throughout this ancient city. No one knew what lay behind its walls. Had the Cultural Revolution never come, its secret might have remained buried forever. Beijing was a city of mysteries. For more than six centuries of imperial rule, countless secrets had been hidden beneath its roofs and behind its walls. So many of them lay concealed within those small, unremarkable siheyuan courtyards. That was Beijing. But today everything was different. The young Red Guards had no taboos, no restraints, no limits. They feared neither Heaven nor Earth. They would even dare drag the highest authority from his throne. As for feudalists, capitalists, revisionists, and every so-called "monster and demon," they were hardly worth mentioning. The Red Guards marched into the quiet courtyard in formation. The courtyard itself was small, but it overflowed with blooming red roses. Anyone who came near could immediately breathe in their rich fragrance. Amid the noise and turmoil of the city, this was a place of rare tranquility—a peaceful refuge, a true earthly paradise. But today, on this very day, that peace was shattered. This little paradise was turned upside down and destroyed. Its mistress herself was about to be sent on the road to the underworld. Like a violent storm, the Red Guards surged into this peaceful sanctuary. Their faces blazed with revolutionary determination and the fierce excitement of youth. Leading them was a young woman named Lin Hui. She was young and attractive. Her hair, tied neatly into a short ponytail, gave her an air of efficiency beyond her years. Her face overflowed with revolutionary zeal, yet there was also a maturity that seemed strangely out of place for someone so young. She wore a green military uniform, with a broad brass-buckled belt fastened tightly around her waist. Her eyes were hard and resolute. Shouting, "Down with the remnants of feudalism!", she smashed open the tightly closed gate of the courtyard. The Red Guards poured inside behind her. She，and the young men and women who followed her，believed with absolute conviction that they were taking part in an unprecedented revolution, one that would surely be written into history by future generations. For that cause, they were willing to dedicate their youth. For the ideals of their Great Leader, they believed they could sacrifice everything—even their own lives. Without a word, the Red Guards kicked open the door of the main house and dragged an elderly woman outside. She wore a flowing white silk robe, with white garments beneath it. Her body was so thin that she seemed almost weightless, as though a passing breeze might carry her away. Though already in the twilight of life, she still carried herself with remarkable grace. The noble bearing upon her face revealed traces of the extraordinary beauty she had once possessed. Her long white hair fell to her waist like strands of silver. A hint of alarm flickered in her eyes, yet it could not conceal the quiet dignity and unwavering resolve that still shone within them. The sight of the old woman startled several of the young Red Guards. For an instant, she seemed less like a living person than a ghost from another age. "Ghost! A ghost!" someone suddenly screamed, turning to run toward the gate. "Nonsense! Where's the ghost?" Lin Hui's sharp voice rang out, stopping the fleeing Red Guard in his tracks. "Take a good look!" she shouted. "This is one of the hidden enemies concealed in the capital of our revolution—a genuine remnant of feudalism! Class struggle is happening right beside us! Isn't this proof enough?" Her voice echoed across the courtyard. "Down with the remnants of feudalism!" "Long live Chairman Mao!" Led by Lin Hui, the Red Guards burst into loud revolutionary slogans. "You feudal leftover!" Lin Hui shouted at the old woman. "You relic of the Qing dynasty! You still dare hide here!" Her powerful voice reverberated throughout the courtyard. At her command, the Red Guards rushed into the house and began searching every room, overturning every corner. Soon they dragged out piles of objects that appeared to be valuable antiques and books, throwing everything into the middle of the courtyard. A bonfire was quickly lit, and the flames eagerly consumed these historical relics and precious works of art, all condemned as part of the "Four Olds." "What is your name?" Lin Hui demanded sternly. "Yehe Nara." The elderly woman answered calmly, firmly pronouncing her family name. Her voice carried neither fear nor hesitation, only quiet dignity. "Yehe Nara? Nonsense! She's lying!"， one of the Red Guards immediately cried. "My mother works for the neighborhood committee," the young man declared. "She knows who this woman really is. She is the former favorite consort of the Guangxu Emperor，the Precious Consort of the Qing Dynasty. Her whole life was paid for with the sweat and blood of working people!" "I am Yehe Nara," the old woman replied with equal firmness. "I am telling the truth." Her quiet attempt to explain only poured oil onto the fire. The young Red Guards were instantly enraged. This remnant of the old feudal order still dared to answer back. She still dared to defend herself. Lin Hui's fury exploded. With a savage kick, she knocked the old woman to the ground. The Red Guards surged forward as one. They unfastened the brass-buckled belts from around their waists and brought them down upon the frail old woman like a driving rainstorm. Very soon, her white garments were stained crimson with blood. The shocking colors were like the red roses blooming throughout the courtyard. In only a few moments, a woman who had once been one of the most celebrated ladies of her age breathed her last. By the time Police Station Chief Tan Yuxi arrived after receiving word of the attack, Consort Zhen was already lying motionless in a pool of blood. She had been dead for some time. Having lived beyond ninety years of age, Consort Zhen had survived Empress Dowager Cixi's attempt to kill her in 1900. She had escaped abroad, endured countless hardships, and survived dangers beyond measure. Unable to overcome her longing for her homeland, she had secretly returned to Beijing to live out the remainder of her life in quiet seclusion. Who could possibly have imagined that, on this day in 1966, after surviving so many calamities and reaching the age of ninety, she would finally die beneath the brass-buckled belts of the Red Guards? Her soul rose gently toward the heavens, seeking the one she had loved all her life— Emperor Guangxu. Meanwhile, the priceless jade ruyi that Emperor Guangxu had once bestowed upon her—the treasured imperial heirloom that had long been preserved within the Qing Palace，was discovered by the Red Guards. Without the slightest hesitation or pity, they hurled it onto the blue brick paving stones of the courtyard. It shattered into countless pieces. Alas... How tragic. How unspeakably sorrowful...
