這點雪算什麼？ 我們得抓緊， 這些老人年齡大了， 萬一有個長短， 我們失去的， 就是再也無法彌補的珍貴口述歷史， 那才是遺憾呢， 你說呢？。」 盧正鋼笑了笑：「妳要是不怕， 我絕對沒有問題， 只是這些天我們不停地到處跑， 妳能挺得住嗎？」 青兒笑了： 「 我沒問題， 倒是你要考慮一下， 是不是要休息一天？」 這一年來，他們已經習慣了這種生活。 走幾十里山路，拜訪一位老人，記錄一段往事，再走幾十里路返回。 有時一天只記下幾百個字，有時卻能得到一些極其珍貴的口述歷史資料。 他們回到屋裡，吃了些昨晚剩下的烤肉和玉米餅， 作為早餐。帶了一壺水。跨包裡裝了幾個玉米餅子，便冒著大雪出發了。 雪很深。每走一步，腳都會陷下去，拔出來非常費力。青兒走在前面，她從小在山裡長大，對林中的道路比盧正鋼熟悉。 一路上，四周靜的嚇人，只有他們踏雪的咯吱聲，偶爾能看見野兔留下的腳印，也能看見狐狸在雪地裡穿行的痕跡。 走了大約一個時辰，前面出現一條冰封的小河，河面覆蓋著厚厚的積雪。 青兒停下腳步，望著遠方，不知道在想什麼。 盧正鋼走到她身邊：「累了？」 青兒搖搖頭，沉默了一會兒，她忽然問： 「正鋼，你還記得第一次認識我，是什麼時候的事？」 盧正鋼愣了一下，隨即笑了。 「當然記得。」 「什麼時候？」 「那場籃球比賽。」 青兒轉過頭看他：「真的？」 「當然是真的。」盧正鋼說： 「那天妳們和清華女籃比賽。最後一分鐘，比分仍然落後一分，大家都覺得沒希望，今天輸定了。 可誰也沒想到，是妳， 像一支利箭， 搶下籃板，從後場一路衝到前場，連過對方兩個後衛，最後三步上籃， 投進一球，贏了。」 青兒忍不住笑了 ：「你記得那麼清楚？」 「那是當然， 太精彩了 ， 現在我腦子裡， 還清楚記得當時的場景， 厲害的青兒， 一球定乾坤， 激動的觀眾湧進球場， 把妳圍了起來，又是握手， 又是擁抱，後來全場有節奏地高喊 ； 青兒，青兒，我們愛你 ，。。。。。。那個場景我一輩子都忘不了 。」 青兒笑了， 盧正鋼也笑了，笑聲在空曠的山林裡傳得很遠。 笑過之後。四周又恢復了令人有些不安的沉靜。 過了一會兒，青兒輕聲說：「那時候真好。」 盧正鋼沒有接話，因為他知道，她說的不是籃球，而是那個還沒有被打成右派的快樂女孩， 那個相信只要努力讀書，就能改變命運的純潔女孩。 他們都沉默了，雪地上只剩下兩人不斷向前的踏雪腳步聲。 又走了一段路，青兒忽然說：「有時候我會想， 如果我當初不整理那些俄國人侵略中國的資料，不研究江東六十四屯慘案， 是不是就不會有後面的那些事。」 盧正鋼停下腳步， 認真地看著她：「妳後悔了嗎？」 青兒沉默，許久，才輕輕搖頭： 「我也不知道， 只是有時候覺得是不是值得？ 那些死去的人，已經死去了五十多年，現在誰還在乎他們 ？」 盧正鋼望著遠方白茫茫的山林， 半天沒有說話， 過了好一會兒， 他才慢慢地， 一個字一個字地說：「妳、在、乎。」 青兒沒有說話。 盧正鋼繼續說：「妳爺爺在乎，那些失去親人的人也在乎。只不過很多人，現在只是不敢說而已。」 風忽然凌厲吹過， 捲起地上的雪粉撲向人臉。 盧正鋼抹去臉上的雪， 接著說：「如果大家都不說，那麼再過幾十年， 一百年，就沒有人知道， 當時真正發生過什麼事情了！」 青兒低著頭，沒有吭聲， 一步一步， 繼續慢慢向前走。 不知道為什麼，她只覺眼眶一熱，淚水便不自覺地流了下來。 就在這時，遠處山坡上忽然傳來一聲長長的狼嚎，淒厲，瘮人，讓人不自覺地感到緊張。 兩人幾乎同時停下腳步，盧正鋼下意識地舉起獵槍，青兒則向聲音傳來的方向望去。 白雪覆蓋的山坡上，隱隱約約出現了一個黑點， 不像是狼， 倒像是一個人，又像是一棵樹。 不過距離太遠，看不清楚。 盧正鋼不由地皺起眉頭：「那是什麼？」 青兒看了很久，緩緩搖頭：「不知道。」可是她心裡忽然生出一種說不出的感覺，那種感覺比聽見狼嚎更可怕，像是有人，正在遠遠地注視著他們。 雪地裡，寒風再次吹了起來，而遠處那個黑點既沒有消失， 也沒有移動， A blast of bitterly cold air struck her face at once. The pine forest before her was covered in snow. It stood in utter silence, breathtakingly beautiful. Just then, she heard the wooden door behind her creak open. Lu Zhenggang stepped outside as well. A hunting rifle rested across his shoulder, a hunting knife hung at his waist, and a short axe was in his hand. Walking over to her, he glanced at the snow and said, "The snow's too deep. It won't be an easy walk today." Qing'er nodded. "It'll probably take us two shichen （hours）to reach Grandma Wang's place." Lu Zhenggang smiled. "Another hard day ahead. Can you handle it?" Qing'er laughed. "I'm fine. The one who should think about taking a day's rest is you." During the past year, they had grown accustomed to this kind of life. They would hike dozens of li through the mountains, visit an elderly survivor, record another fragment of the past, and then walk dozens more li back home. Sometimes an entire day's journey yielded only a few hundred words of notes. At other times, it brought them historical testimony of immeasurable value. They returned to the cabin and ate the leftover roasted venison and corn cakes from the night before as breakfast. They filled a water flask, tucked several corn cakes inside their coats, and set off once again. The snow was deep. Every step sank into it, and every step had to be pulled free with effort. Qing'er walked in front. Having grown up in these mountains, she knew the forest paths far better than Lu Zhenggang did. All around them, the wilderness was unnervingly quiet. Apart from the crunch of their boots pressing into the snow, they occasionally spotted rabbit tracks or the winding footprints left behind by foxes. After walking for about an hour, they came upon a small frozen river, its surface buried beneath a thick blanket of snow. Qing'er stopped and gazed into the distance, lost in thought. Lu Zhenggang walked over beside her. "Tired?" Qing'er shook her head. After a moment of silence, she suddenly asked, "Zhenggang... do you remember the first time you ever saw me?" Qing'er turned to look at him. "Really?" "Of course I remember," Lu Zhenggang replied. "That day your team was playing against the Tsinghua University women's basketball team. There was only one minute left, and you were still trailing by one point. Everyone thought the game was over—that your team was certain to lose. But no one expected you to burst forward like an arrow. You grabbed the rebound, sprinted the full length of the court from the backcourt, dribbled past two defenders, and finished with a layup. That single basket won the game." Qing'er could not help laughing. "You remember it that clearly?" "Of course I do. It was incredible. Even now, I can still see the whole scene in my mind as clearly as if it happened yesterday. The amazing Qing'er—one basket decided the entire game. The whole arena began chanting in rhythm: 'Qing'er! Qing'er! We love you!' I'll never forget that moment for the rest of my life." Qing'er laughed. Lu Zhenggang laughed as well, and their laughter drifted far through the silent mountains. When the laughter faded, the forest once again returned to an uneasy stillness. After a while, Qing'er spoke softly. "Those were wonderful days." Lu Zhenggang did not answer. Because he knew she was not talking about basketball. She was talking about the cheerful young woman she had once been before she was branded a Rightist—the innocent university student who had believed that, as long as she studied hard, she could change her own destiny. They both fell silent. Only the steady crunch of their footsteps in the snow accompanied them as they continued forward. After walking another stretch, Qing'er suddenly said, "Sometimes I wonder... if I had never gathered those materials about Russia's invasion of China, if I had never researched the massacre of the Sixty-Four Villages East of the River, would everything that happened afterward have been avoided?" Lu Zhenggang stopped and looked at her seriously. "Do you regret it?" Qing'er remained silent for a long time before gently shaking her head. "I don't know. Sometimes I simply wonder whether it was worth it. Those people have been dead for more than fifty years. Who still cares about them now?" Lu Zhenggang gazed across the endless snow-covered forest. For a long time, he said nothing. Then, very slowly, emphasizing each word, he answered, "You... do." Qing'er remained silent. Lu Zhenggang continued, "Your grandfather cared. The people who lost their loved ones cared. It's only that many of them no longer dare to speak." A fierce gust of wind suddenly swept through the mountains, lifting loose snow and hurling it into their faces. Brushing the snow from his face, Lu Zhenggang went on, "If no one speaks, then fifty years from now... a hundred years from now... no one will know what truly happened." Qing'er lowered her head without saying a word and continued walking slowly, one step after another. For reasons she herself could not explain, her eyes suddenly grew warm, and tears quietly began to roll down her cheeks. Just then, a long wolf's howl echoed from a distant hillside. It was shrill and eerie, sending an involuntary chill through anyone who heard it. Both of them stopped almost at the same instant. Lu Zhenggang instinctively raised his hunting rifle, while Qing'er turned toward the direction from which the howl had come. On the snow-covered hillside, a tiny dark figure could be seen in the distance. It did not look like a wolf. It looked more like a person. Or perhaps only a tree. The distance was too great to tell. Lu Zhenggang frowned. "What is that?" Qing'er stared at it for a long time before slowly shaking her head. "I don't know." Yet an indescribable feeling suddenly rose within her.It was even more frightening than the wolf's howl. It felt as though someone, far away, was watching them. The wind swept across the snow once more. The dark figure on the distant hillside neither disappeared nor moved. It stood there as motionless as a wooden post, as though quietly waiting...
