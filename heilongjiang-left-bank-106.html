在北京火車站永遠是人滿為患的巨大候車室裡，灰心至極的青兒，好不容易找了一個空位子坐下。 她腦子裡一片空白。看著像螞蟻一樣亂哄哄來來往往的旅客， 候車室的椅子上坐滿了人，甚至連地上都坐滿了，讓走路的人無處下腳。她感到頭暈， 噁心要吐。 自從被打成右派以來， 她在北大的最後幾天， 真是度日如年， 她就像是一個活死人， 機械麻木地辦理離開大學的手續。 不用上課了，沒人理， 沒人問，她已經不屬於這裡， She had searched through materials in the library and taken notes in order to understand the truth of the past. What sacred law had she violated? Collecting damaging materials for anti-Soviet purposes. Narrow bourgeois nationalism. She could accept none of those enormous political labels. Did exposing the crimes against humanity committed in China by imperial Tsarist Russia mean that she opposed the present-day Union of Soviet Socialist Republics? She turned the question over in her mind again and again, but still could not accept it. Yet reality was reality. Who cared whether she understood or accepted it? Once the organization declared that she was anti-Soviet, then anti-Soviet she was. Whether she understood or accepted the judgment was no longer the organization’s concern. That morning, the sky was dark and overcast, wrapped in mist. Carrying an old canvas suitcase and wearing a backpack filled with books and notebooks, Qing’er arrived alone at Beijing Railway Station. Inside the enormous waiting hall of Beijing Railway Station, which always seemed overcrowded, the utterly disheartened Qing’er finally managed to find an empty seat. Her mind was completely blank. She watched the noisy travelers moving back and forth like swarming ants. Every chair in the waiting hall was occupied, and even the floor was crowded with people, leaving almost nowhere to place one’s feet. She felt dizzy and nauseated, as though she might vomit. Ever since she had been labeled a Rightist, her final days at Peking University had felt endless. She had become like a living corpse, mechanically and numbly completing the procedures for leaving the university. She no longer attended classes. No one spoke to her, and no one asked about her. She no longer belonged there. She had been discarded like a piece of rubbish. During those days, she had no desire to eat properly. She bought a few steamed buns from the dining hall and left them in her dormitory, taking a bite whenever hunger became unbearable. She did not want to appear again in the student dining hall she had once loved so much. She could no longer endure the coldness, the hostile glances, and the whispered conversations around her. Those who had once cheered for her on the basketball court, those who had once discussed problems with her in the library, those who had competed to fetch her meals or save her a seat in hopes of winning her attention and affection, and those who had once tried every possible way to approach and please her now avoided her as though she carried the plague. Qing’er did not blame them. This was reality. In China at that time, landlords, rich peasants, counterrevolutionaries, bad elements, and Rightists were all placed within the category of enemies of the people. How could anyone dare approach her and risk being implicated? Qing’er blamed no one and hated no one. She simply could not understand. Why? How had she changed from one of the people into an enemy in a single day? She was only a second-year university student. What kind of enemy could she possibly be? She could not understand it. She truly could not understand. While she sat alone in a daze, her thoughts numb, a familiar figure appeared in the distance. Qing’er’s heart trembled. It was him—Lu Zhenggang. Lu Zhenggang saw Qing’er as well and hurried toward her. Qing’er then noticed that a middle-aged woman had come with him. Her hair was streaked with gray, her face was gentle and kind, and she was smiling at Qing’er. “This is my mother,” Lu Zhenggang said by way of introduction. “When she learned what had happened to you, she insisted on coming to see you off.” Qing’er was deeply moved. Somewhere in her heart, she had quietly hoped that Lu Zhenggang might come to see her off, but the fact that even his mother had come was completely beyond anything she had expected. Lu’s mother held Qing’er’s hands tightly and studied her face for nearly a full minute. Then she sighed and said, “What a fine child you are. Zhenggang told me what happened. Peking University handled your case wrongly. Don’t lose heart. Go home and rest for a while. I will do everything I can to help you and see whether there is still any chance of changing the decision.” At Lu’s mother’s words, the tears that Qing’er had held back for so long suddenly spilled down her face. She looked at the woman’s visibly ill and weary features, overwhelmed by emotion and unable to find the words to express herself. In the end, she could only bow deeply before her. Lu Zhenggang handed Qing’er the string bag he was carrying. Inside were fruit bread, apples, a whole length of Beijing garlic sausage, and several bottles of soda. Qing’er tried to refuse, but Lu’s mother said, “Take them, child. This is a small token from Gang’er. Don’t lose heart. Be strong. Everyone encounters hardships in life. As long as you hold fast to what you believe, there will always be a way forward.” Lu Zhenggang and his mother accompanied Qing’er all the way onto the train. They found her seat, helped stow her luggage, and remained with her until the train was about to depart. Only then did Lu Zhenggang reluctantly release her hand and say with great seriousness, “Don’t be afraid. Wait for me. I will come and find you.” Qing’er answered just as seriously, “Lu Zhenggang, don’t be foolish. I am already a Rightist. I cannot drag you down with me. Don’t concern yourself with me anymore. Take good care of your mother. She does not look well.” The train began to move. Through tear-filled eyes, Qing’er watched Lu Zhenggang running alongside the carriage, waving to her again and again. Qing’er returned to her seat and opened the small paper packet that Lu’s mother had slipped into her hand before leaving the train. Inside was a ten-yuan banknote. Written on the wrapping were a few words: “Child, be strong. The sky will not fall.” Two days later, after changing trains several times, Qing’er returned to her hometown. What awaited her was not flowers or applause, but an official notice. The production team had already received the document sent down by the county authorities: Li Qing’er, a Rightist student from Peking University, was to be sent back to her home village and placed under supervised labor reform. The bad news had arrived even before she did. Almost everyone in the village already knew. The moment Qing’er reached home, many people came running to watch the spectacle. The courtyard of her family home was crowded inside and out. Some had come out of genuine concern, some merely out of curiosity, and others because they took pleasure in her misfortune. As Qing’er made her way through the crowd, she heard people whispering. “Wasn’t she studying at a university in Beijing? Why has she come back?” “I heard she made some kind of mistake.” “What kind of mistake?” “Who knows?” “Whatever it was, it can’t have been anything good.” “Otherwise, why would they have sent her back?” The voices were not loud, but every word reached Qing’er clearly. She could only lower her head, pretend she had heard nothing, and hurry into the house. That night, her father sat on the edge of the heated brick bed, smoking his long-stemmed pipe until dawn without saying a single word. Her mother secretly wiped away her tears. The daughter she loved most had once been her pride, the pride of the entire village. Now she had returned wearing the label of a Rightist. She did not know how to comfort her daughter, nor did she know how to face what had happened. The very next day, the production team leader summoned Qing’er and assigned her to labor—hauling manure, cutting grass, digging drainage ditches. They were all heavy, dirty jobs normally done by able-bodied men, work that few women in the village were ever asked to perform. Qing’er neither complained nor shirked her duties. Whatever task the production team leader assigned, she accepted it without argument and completed it quietly, leaving him with nothing to criticize. Yet she soon discovered that what hurt most was not the labor itself, but the strange looks people gave her. People used to say, “She’s the university student from Beijing. She’s the golden phoenix who flew out of our village. One day she’ll become a university professor.” Now they said instead, “That Rightist student. I hear there’s something wrong with her thinking. She studied so much that she studied herself into trouble. Too much education isn’t always a good thing.” One day, while she was washing clothes by the river, several village women passed by. One of them deliberately raised her voice and said, “See? What’s the use of girls studying so much? In the end, she studied herself into becoming a Rightist!” The women burst into laughter. Qing’er’s hands trembled for a moment, then she lowered her head and continued washing her clothes. She did not utter a single word, but tears of humiliation flowed down her cheeks beyond her control. That evening, she walked alone to a small hill outside the village. Beyond it stretched the endless Changbai Mountains, their forests bathed in the quiet light of the setting sun. She suddenly found herself thinking of Beijing—of Weiming Lake, of the university library, of the basketball court. Those places that had once seemed so close now felt as though they belonged to another lifetime. Just then, she heard footsteps behind her. It was her father. The old man walked to her side and handed her something. It was the hunting knife he had carried his entire life. The worn leather sheath had been polished smooth by countless years of use. “Take it,” her father said. There aren’t so many cruel tongues there.” A breeze swept across the hillside, stirring the dry grass. Qing’er gripped the hunting knife tightly and remained silent for a long time. At that moment, she understood that there were some places to which she could never return. Late that night, after everyone in the village had fallen asleep, Qing’er quietly shouldered her backpack, took her hunting knife, gathered her notebooks, and slipped away from home. Moonlight fell softly upon her as the distant Changbai Mountains stood like a silent black giant before her. Without looking back, she walked step by step into the depths of the forest. She did not know what the future held, nor where her path would ultimately lead. But she knew one thing with certainty—from this day forward, she would have to rely on no one but herself.
