In Dream of the Red Chamber, Miaoyu ranks sixth among the Twelve Beauties of Jinling, after Lin Daiyu, Xue Baochai, Jia Yuanchun, Jia Tanchun, and Shi Xiangyun. Her ranking is highly unusual. She is a true ice beauty, truly like a block of ice, coldness radiating from her body, keeping everyone a thousand miles away. Other people all think Miaoyu strange in temperament and difficult to approach. Baoyu is different. He not only likes her and draws near to her; he also understands her deeply and respects her. Because he knows that when a person forces herself to live with such painful effort merely to preserve purity, aloofness, and pride, it often means that deep inside, she is more afraid than anyone else—afraid that the filth of this world will contaminate her, and destroy her. But things go against her wishes. After the shelter of the Jia family collapses with a crash, the purity and aloof pride that Miaoyu has always regarded as life itself vanish completely. She is humiliated and defiled more cruelly than anyone else. Miaoyu originally came from an excellent family background. Her ancestors belonged to a scholarly and official family in Suzhou. Her parents loved her to the utmost, sparing no effort for her. From childhood, she lived in silk and fine food, learned to read and write, and attained a very high level of cultivation in the traditional arts of music, chess, tea, and flower arrangement. She was extraordinarily beautiful and possessed exceptional talent. That is why Cao Xueqin gave her such a lofty judgment in the book: "Her temperament is as beautiful as an orchid; her talent is more fragrant than an immortal's." But Heaven is fair. It does not give every good thing to one person alone. Some say that Miaoyu's fate was unfortunate. As a child, she was often ill. Her parents searched everywhere for famous physicians, yet none could cure her. Later, the family asked someone to tell her fortune, and they were told that her life was fragile; if she did not enter the Buddhist gate, she might not live long. Her parents could not bear to let their daughter suffer. For this reason, they once tried to use substitutes, sending others into religious life on her behalf to pray for her. But none of it worked. And so, loving their daughter desperately yet utterly helpless, her parents had no choice but to send her to a nunnery when she was still very young, where she practiced as a Buddhist novice while keeping her hair. Yet Miaoyu herself had never truly seen through the red dust of the world, nor had she truly entered the Buddhist gate. Later, after both of her parents passed away, Miaoyu found herself alone in the world. With no one left to rely on, she could only leave home with her two elderly nursemaids and a young maidservant and seek refuge at Xuanmu Panxiang Monastery (玄墓蟠香寺) in Suzhou, where she continued her Buddhist practice while keeping her hair. Later, accompanied by her master, she traveled to the capital under the pretext of paying homage to sacred Buddhist relics—sites associated with Guanyin, the Bodhisattva of Compassion, and the treasured palm-leaf Buddhist scriptures preserved there. They eventually settled at Muni Nunnery (牟尼庵) on the western outskirts of the imperial city. Muni Nunnery became Miaoyu's temporary home after leaving Suzhou. Unlike ordinary convents for Buddhist women, it was small and quiet, intended primarily for women of noble birth who had entered religious life. It was not a place of hardship, but a secluded sanctuary for ladies from distinguished families. Some time later, Miaoyu's master fell gravely ill. Before passing away, she left Miaoyu one final instruction: do not return to Suzhou. Remain in the capital and wait for your destined opportunity. How, then, did Miaoyu eventually enter the Jia household and come to reside in Longcui Nunnery (櫳翠庵) within the Grand View Garden? Cao Xueqin tells the story in the original novel. "Lin Zhixiao's wife came to report: 'We have already found ten young Buddhist and Daoist novices, and twenty new robes have been prepared. There is also a young woman practicing Buddhism while keeping her hair. She is a native of Suzhou, born into a scholarly family of officials. She was sickly from childhood. Her family tried every possible substitute ritual, but nothing worked. Only after she herself entered religious life did her health improve. She is now eighteen years old, with the religious name Miaoyu. Both of her parents have passed away. She is attended only by two elderly nursemaids and one young maid. She is highly accomplished in literature, has no need to study the Buddhist scriptures further, and is remarkably beautiful. Hearing that the capital possessed sacred relics of Guanyin and ancient Buddhist palm-leaf manuscripts, she followed her master here last year and is now living at Muni Nunnery outside the western gate. Her master, who was highly skilled in divination, passed away last winter. Miaoyu had intended to escort her master's coffin back to Suzhou, but before dying, her master instructed her: "Your future does not lie in returning home. Remain here quietly, and in time your destiny will reveal itself." So she never returned to Suzhou.'" Before the report was even finished, Lady Wang said, "If that is the case, why don't we invite her here?" Lin Zhixiao's wife replied, "We did ask her. She answered, 'The great aristocratic houses always rely upon their rank and power to overawe others. I will never go.'" Lady Wang smiled. "Since she herself comes from an official family, it is only natural that she should possess some pride. Then let us send her a formal invitation." Lin Zhixiao's wife withdrew at once, instructing the household secretary to prepare an official invitation. The following day, a carriage and attendants were dispatched to escort Miaoyu with full ceremony. From this passage, one immediately sees how proud and self-respecting Miaoyu was. Only after the Jia family followed the highest etiquette—issuing an official written invitation instead of a casual summons—did she finally agree to enter the Grand View Garden. Throughout the history of the Jia household, this was an honor granted to no other monk or nun. It was an extraordinary gesture of respect. Miaoyu's residence inside the Grand View Garden was called Longcui Nunnery. Every aspect of its design perfectly reflected her character. Whether its secluded location or its carefully arranged surroundings, it remained the Garden's only true sanctuary—a small island of Buddhist tranquility amid the luxurious world of the Jia family. Hidden among rocks and ancient trees, Longcui Nunnery possessed an atmosphere of profound peace and silence. It was also the Garden's only residence devoted to a woman living the religious life. Yet the nunnery stood within the very grounds where all the young ladies of the Jia family lived. Symbolically, this reveals that although Miaoyu had entered Buddhism, she had never completely severed herself from the secular world. At the same time, her residence stood apart from the other girls' quarters, quietly expressing her determination to remain spiritually above ordinary society and apart from its worldly attachments. The most distinctive feature of Longcui Nunnery was its grove of red plum blossoms. In Chinese culture, the plum blossom is admired for blooming in the snow and surviving the bitter cold. It symbolizes purity, resilience, and an unwillingness to compromise with the world. The crimson blossoms surrounding the nunnery perfectly mirrored Miaoyu's own character—proud, unyielding, and unwilling to be stained by worldly corruption. Yet their deep red color also hinted at something else. Although Miaoyu had entered religious life, she had never completely severed her ties with the human world. Beneath the robes of a Buddhist devotee still lived a young woman whose heart had never entirely escaped earthly emotions. Why did Miaoyu leave her quiet religious life in Suzhou, travel hundreds of miles to the imperial capital, and finally settle in the Grand View Garden? Cao Xueqin leaves two important clues. The first is that while living in Suzhou, Miaoyu's extraordinary literary talent and uncompromising personality offended certain powerful local figures. Because of her pride and refusal to yield, she eventually found herself unwelcome there. To escape their harassment, she followed her master to Beijing under the respectable pretext of paying homage to sacred Buddhist relics and ancient palm-leaf scriptures. The second clue lies in her master's final prophecy. Before her death, the old nun told Miaoyu: "Your life should not return to your native place. Remain here quietly. In time, your destiny will naturally unfold." Her master foresaw that returning to Suzhou would bring disaster. Instead, she instructed Miaoyu to remain in the capital and await the fate prepared for her. Entering the Jia family and taking up residence in the Grand View Garden marked the beginning of that destiny. For a time, the Garden sheltered her. In the end, it also became the place from which her tragedy began. When Miaoyu entered the Grand View Garden, she was not an ordinary Buddhist nun who had abandoned everything. She arrived as a woman practicing Buddhism while retaining her secular identity. She was accompanied by two elderly nursemaids and a young maidservant who attended to her daily needs. Although dressed in grey Buddhist robes, she kept her long hair and continued to live much as she had when she was the cherished daughter of a wealthy official family. This unusual way of life reveals an important truth. Miaoyu had entered Buddhism, but she had never experienced the hardship and simplicity that genuine monastic life demanded. Physically she belonged to the Buddhist world; materially she still lived as an aristocratic young lady. This perfectly echoes the prophetic verse Cao Xueqin later assigns to her: "She longs for purity, yet cannot truly be pure. She speaks of emptiness, yet has not fully emptied herself. Alas, a body as precious as gold and jade Will in the end sink into the mire." The Jia family welcomed Miaoyu into Longcui Nunnery with extraordinary courtesy and respect. Yet behind that honor lay a profound irony. A woman who least belonged to the secular world was brought into the very heart of one of its greatest aristocratic households. Longcui Nunnery, with its quiet courtyards and flowering plum trees, gave Miaoyu the illusion of a paradise beyond worldly dust. But because she entered it still surrounded by servants, refined possessions, and an uncompromising devotion to elegance and purity, the sanctuary could never truly protect her. When the mighty Jia family finally collapsed, the little nunnery collapsed with it. Thus Miaoyu lived between two worlds. She belonged completely to neither. She had left ordinary society behind, yet had never fully entered the Buddhist path. She stood suspended between the secular and the sacred, unable to return to one and unable to embrace the other. That is why her loneliness ran deeper than that of almost anyone else in Dream of the Red Chamber. Perhaps no episode reveals Miaoyu's true character more vividly than Chapter Forty-One, the famous scene in which Jia Baoyu visits Longcui Nunnery to drink tea. Through what appears to be an ordinary tea gathering, Cao Xueqin quietly reveals Miaoyu's obsessive pursuit of purity, her astonishing family background, her aristocratic pride, and—most importantly—the profound irony that will eventually determine her fate. The episode in Chapter Forty-One describes Grandmother Jia and Granny Liu visiting Longcui Nunnery for tea. What appears at first glance to be a simple social call gradually reveals Miaoyu's astonishing wealth, her obsession with refinement, and the almost unbelievable treasures that surrounded her. Although she lived as a Buddhist devotee, the objects she owned amazed even members of the enormously wealthy Jia family. These priceless antiques quietly confirm once again that Miaoyu was no ordinary nun. She remained, in every sense, the daughter of an elite aristocratic family. To better understand Miaoyu, it is worth reading part of Cao Xueqin's original description. Grandmother Jia and the others entered Longcui Nunnery after finishing their meal. Seeing the carefully tended flowers and trees, Grandmother Jia smiled. "Those who cultivate themselves always have time to care for such things. This place is even more beautiful than the rest of the Garden." Miaoyu welcomed them respectfully into the meditation hall. Grandmother Jia laughed. "We've just finished eating meat and drinking wine. There are Bodhisattvas here, so we shouldn't offend them. Let us simply sit for a while and have a cup of your finest tea." Miaoyu immediately prepared the tea herself. Baoyu watched every movement carefully. She personally carried an exquisitely carved lacquer tray decorated with dragons and longevity motifs. Upon it rested a tiny covered porcelain tea cup. Grandmother Jia glanced at it and said, "I don't drink Liu'an tea." Miaoyu smiled. "I know. This is Old Gentleman Eyebrow Tea (Laojunmei)." Grandmother Jia accepted it and asked, "What water did you use?" Miaoyu replied quietly, "Rainwater collected last year." Grandmother Jia drank only half the cup before handing it to Granny Liu. "Taste it." Granny Liu emptied the cup in one gulp and laughed. "It's certainly good tea, but it's rather weak. It would taste better brewed stronger." Everyone burst into laughter. The scene is humorous. Yet beneath its humor lies one of the novel's sharpest social contrasts. The tiny porcelain cup used by Grandmother Jia was no ordinary teacup. It was a Chenghua imperial porcelain cup, one of the finest products of the Ming imperial kilns and an object of extraordinary rarity and value. Later, Miaoyu invited Baochai and Daiyu into her private room for what she called a more intimate cup of tea. The tea vessels she selected were even more astonishing. Baochai received a Paojia (瓟斝)—an ancient wine vessel treasured since the Jin Dynasty and said to bear an inscription by the famous aristocrat Wang Kai. Daiyu was served in a Dianxi cup (點犀䀉), an exceptionally rare vessel carved from precious jade. Then Baoyu entered. At first, Miaoyu handed him her own personal Green Jade Cup (綠玉斗). This immediately caught Baoyu's attention. In the social conventions of eighteenth-century China, for an unmarried woman to offer a man the cup she herself normally used carried unmistakable emotional significance. Realizing the delicate implication, Miaoyu quietly changed her mind. Instead, she replaced it with another vessel，an extraordinary tea cup painstakingly carved from an entire section of twisted bamboo root, known in the novel as the "Nine-Curved, Ten-Ring, One-Hundred-and-Twenty-Jointed Bamboo Root Cup." Even the immensely wealthy Jia family possessed nothing comparable. These objects were valuable not merely because they were expensive.They were cultural treasures. Each represented centuries of history, craftsmanship, and aristocratic taste. Together they reveal something that Cao Xueqin never states directly. Miaoyu's family background, at the height of its prosperity, may well have rivaled that of the Jia family itself. Yet the most dramatic conflict in this entire episode has nothing to do with priceless antiques. It lies in Miaoyu's attitude toward Granny Liu. Grandmother Jia had casually handed her own cup to the old country woman. Granny Liu drank from it without a second thought. For Granny Liu, it was merely a cup of tea. For Miaoyu, it became something entirely different. After the guests had gone, Miaoyu quietly ordered, "Set that Chenghua cup outside. Do not bring it back in." Simply because Granny Liu，a poor peasant woman，had drunk from it once, the priceless imperial porcelain had become permanently contaminated in Miaoyu's eyes. She would rather discard it than ever use it again. Baoyu understood Miaoyu's temperament better than anyone else. Seeing that such a priceless antique was about to be discarded, he gently suggested, "It would be a pity to waste such a fine cup. Why not give it to the old lady? She could sell it and live comfortably for quite some time." Because the suggestion came from Baoyu, Miaoyu hesitated before reluctantly agreeing. Even then she said proudly, "Fortunately, I had never drunk from it myself. Had I used it, I would rather smash it than give it away." After Granny Liu left, Miaoyu's obsession with cleanliness did not end there. She even considered the places where Granny Liu had walked and sat to be polluted. Several buckets of water were brought to wash the ground thoroughly, cleansing every spot the old country woman had touched. Her pursuit of refinement extended far beyond porcelain and antiques. Even the water used for making tea had to meet standards that ordinary people could scarcely imagine. When Baochai and Daiyu were invited into her private room, Daiyu asked casually, "Is this also rainwater collected last year?" Miaoyu gave a faint, almost disdainful smile. "For someone like you, how can you not taste the difference? You are, after all, quite a vulgar person." She continued, "This is snow gathered from plum blossoms five years ago while I was living at Xuanmu Panxiang Monastery. I collected enough to fill an entire ghost-face green porcelain jar. I treasured it so much that I buried it underground and waited until this summer before opening it. I have used it only once before. This is only the second time. How could last year's rainwater possibly possess such delicate lightness?" "You are actually such a vulgar person，you cannot even tell one water from another." Only Miaoyu would dare say such words to Lin Daiyu. Daiyu was universally acknowledged as the most gifted and refined young woman in the entire novel. Yet in Miaoyu's eyes, even Daiyu failed to appreciate the highest level of elegance. The scene perfectly reveals Miaoyu's absolute confidence in her own aesthetic standards，and the extraordinary pride that accompanied them. Not everyone in the Grand View Garden admired such behavior. Among those who understood Miaoyu best was Xing Xiuyan, once her impoverished companion and, in many ways, her student. Xiuyan was one of the Garden's clearest-minded and most easygoing young women. Her judgment of Miaoyu is famous: "She is eccentric and completely out of step with the world... neither truly a nun nor truly worldly; neither properly feminine nor masculine. What kind of life is that?" No one understood Miaoyu better. Xiuyan recognized that Miaoyu's pursuit of purity had gradually become an obsession. Miaoyu believed she had transcended the secular world. In reality, she remained tightly bound by two invisible chains: her obsession with purity, and her pride in her noble birth. She despised worldly society, yet depended upon the Jia family's worldly wealth for protection. She sought spiritual emptiness, yet remained deeply attached to distinctions of tea, water, antiques, taste, and social rank. Her heart had never truly become free. Here, Cao Xueqin quietly plants one of the greatest ironies in the entire novel. In Chapter Forty-One, Miaoyu shows almost unbearable disgust toward Granny Liu. Much later, after the Jia family collapses, fate returns that contempt with astonishing cruelty. Granny Liu，the poor country woman whom Miaoyu considered too filthy even to touch her porcelain，becomes the rescuer. She sells her own possessions, travels a great distance, and ultimately saves Qiaojie, Wang Xifeng's daughter, from being sold into a brothel. Miaoyu, however... The aristocratic lady who prided herself on remaining above the dust of the world…， the woman who had devoted her entire life to preserving absolute purity…， finds no one willing or able to save her. She becomes the one abandoned to the darkest forces of society. What a merciless irony. The woman who could not tolerate Granny Liu touching a teacup... who ordered even the ground beneath the old woman's feet to be washed clean... is eventually seized one moonless night by ruthless bandits, overcome with drugged incense, carried away by force, and delivered into the hands of the very brutality she had spent her whole life trying to avoid. Her body is violated. Her spirit is shattered. The prophecy written long before finally comes true. "In the end, she sinks into the mire." Could there be a more tragic fulfillment of Cao Xueqin's warning? Even after settling in the Grand View Garden, Miaoyu continued to seal herself away from the people around her. She refused to associate with those whom she considered vulgar. While the other girls gathered to compose poetry, she remained detached. While they wandered through the Garden admiring flowers, she stayed alone inside her nunnery, quietly preparing tea. She treated others with courtesy, yet always with an unmistakable distance that often bordered on severity. She discarded a priceless antique simply because Granny Liu had touched it. Nor did she show much deference to the wealthy masters and ladies of the Jia household. To most people, Miaoyu seemed merely strange. Baoyu, however, saw something entirely different. He himself had never belonged comfortably to the ordinary world. Others pursued official careers, wealth, and social success. He instinctively rejected them. Others spoke endlessly of rank, reputation, and practical ambition. He regarded such things with quiet contempt. From birth, Baoyu possessed an unspoken resistance toward the values of the world around him. It was precisely for this reason that Miaoyu gradually began to notice him. First came admiration. Then affection. She discovered that this young aristocrat was unlike the others. During the famous tea gathering at Longcui Nunnery, Miaoyu brought out the treasures she had guarded for many years. She served Baochai and Daiyu with priceless antique cups. Yet when Baoyu arrived, she instinctively offered him the Green Jade Cup she herself normally used. Even Baoyu paused in surprise. He immediately understood that this was no ordinary gesture of hospitality. In the strict social conventions of the Qing Dynasty, for an unmarried woman to hand a man her own personal cup carried unmistakable emotional meaning. But Miaoyu was not an ordinary woman. Nor was her feeling toward Baoyu merely romantic affection. What she truly cherished was the part of Baoyu that refused to surrender to worldly values. She believed he belonged to the same lonely world that she inhabited. Both of them were outsiders. Both were strangers within society. Both possessed souls too uncompromising to fit comfortably into ordinary life. Perhaps, among everyone in the Grand View Garden, Baoyu was the only person who came close to understanding her. Yet even here, Miaoyu's tragedy surpassed that of Lin Daiyu. Daiyu possessed Baoyu's love. Miaoyu possessed only his understanding. Those two things are not the same. She was too proud. Too uncompromising. Too obsessed with preserving an impossible purity. Gradually, she transformed herself into an isolated island. She refused to approach others.She refused to allow others to approach her. She spent her entire life defending the purity and dignity she believed remained within her, as though standing alone against an entire corrupt world. Yet in the universe created by Cao Xueqin, those who remain the purest often suffer the cruelest fate. Then the Jia family collapsed.The Grand View Garden disappeared. One after another, the young women who had once written poetry together met tragic endings. Some died from illness. Some took their own lives. Some were destroyed by abuse and misfortune. Miaoyu also lost the final shelter protecting her. Cao Xueqin never explicitly tells us what happened afterward.His silence is deliberate. Sometimes what remains unwritten is more terrifying than what is described openly. The clues scattered throughout the novel are already enough. Many traditional commentators, including the early Zhiyanzhai annotations and numerous later scholars of Dream of the Red Chamber, believe that Miaoyu was abducted by bandits and ultimately violated. For a woman whose entire life had been devoted to preserving absolute purity, no fate could have been more devastating. Because the thing Miaoyu treasured most was never her life. It was her spiritual dignity. Her purity. Her refusal to become part of a corrupt world. Reality destroyed precisely that. Whenever Baoyu later remembered Miaoyu, he thought of the snowy day at Longcui Nunnery. She stood there in plain white robes among the falling snow, motionless as a sculpture. Her beauty was breathtaking. Her expression remained distant. Cold. Untouchable. Like ice. At that moment, the Grand View Garden had not yet fallen. Everyone believed those peaceful days would continue forever. No one realized they were witnessing the last quiet season of Miaoyu's life. Only much later did Baoyu finally understand. Miaoyu had never truly been untouched by the dust of the world. She had simply spent her entire life struggling to protect the last fragment of purity still remaining within her. And even that small wish…， was denied her. Thus the proudest, purest, and most uncompromising woman in Dream of the Red Chamber ultimately met one of its dirtiest, cruelest, and most humiliating destinies. Who should bear the blame? Cao Xueqin never answers. He leaves the question to every reader. Finally, let us read the prophetic verse that Cao Xueqin wrote for Miaoyu in Chapter Five, in the Register of the Twelve Beauties of Jinling in the Land of Illusion. Its philosophy remains profoundly moving. "The World Will Not Accept Her" Her grace is fragrant as an orchid; Her gifts surpass those of immortals. Born with a lonely spirit, She is too extraordinary to be understood. She rejects rich food as coarse, Despises silks and luxury as vulgar. Yet those who rise too high invite envy; Those who seek excessive purity are rejected by the world. Alas， She will grow old beneath the lonely lamp of an ancient temple, Having wasted the springtime that once belonged in crimson chambers. In the end, against her deepest wish, She will still fall into the dust and filth of the mortal world. Like flawless white jade sinking into mud... Why should noble young gentlemen lament that they never won her heart? (To be continued)
