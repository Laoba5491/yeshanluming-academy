在賈寶玉心裡，大觀園裡那麼多女孩子，若論模樣標致，晴雯未必第一；若論溫柔體貼，她也遠不如襲人；可若論那股鮮活氣，那股像火一樣燒人的靈氣，卻沒有任何人比得過晴雯。 她像一把雪亮的小刀，鋒利，耀眼，漂亮，卻也最容易折斷。 寶玉第一次真正注意晴雯，是在很多年前。那時賈母看她模樣生得好，針線又伶俐，便賞給了寶玉。從此，她便進了怡紅院。 晴雯生得「風流靈巧」，眉眼有些像林黛玉，卻比黛玉更多一分潑辣。她不像襲人那樣處處小心，也不像麝月那樣穩重，她說話尖利，性子傲，心裡有什麼，嘴上便說什麼。 也正因如此，她在賈府得罪的人太多了。 那些婆子們恨她； 小丫頭們怕她；  就連在怡紅院裡，也有不少人心中並不喜歡她， 雖然沒有人明說 。 可寶玉偏偏最疼她，因為寶玉知道，晴雯雖然嘴硬，心卻最乾淨。

In Jia Baoyu's heart, among all the girls of the Grand View Garden, Qingwen was perhaps not the most beautiful. Nor was she as gentle and considerate as Xiren. But when it came to vitality—that blazing spirit that burned like fire—no one could compare with Qingwen. She was like a gleaming little knife: sharp, dazzling, beautiful, and yet the easiest to break. Baoyu first truly noticed Qingwen many years earlier. Impressed by her beauty and her remarkable skill with needlework, Grandmother Jia assigned her to attend Baoyu. From that day on, Qingwen became one of the maids of the Happy Red Court. Qingwen was described as graceful, spirited, and exceptionally clever. Her features bore some resemblance to Lin Daiyu's, yet she possessed an extra measure of boldness that Daiyu lacked. She was nothing like Xiren, who was cautious in everything she did, nor like Sheyue, who was calm and dependable. Qingwen spoke bluntly, carried herself proudly, and never concealed what was on her mind. For that very reason, she offended far too many people in the Jia household. The old serving women resented her; the younger maids were afraid of her; even within the Happy Red Court, many people secretly disliked her, although few ever dared to say so openly. Yet Baoyu cherished her above all the others, because he knew that although Qingwen's tongue was sharp, her heart was the purest of them all.

The old serving women resented her; the younger maids were afraid of her; even within the Happy Red Court, many people secretly disliked her, although few ever dared to say so openly. Yet Baoyu cherished her above all the others, because he knew that although Qingwen's tongue was sharp, her heart was the purest of them all. One night, Baoyu returned home drunk and accidentally burned a hole in his Sparrow-Gold Fur Cloak. It was an extraordinarily precious garment, a tribute from Russia, and no one in the entire Jia Mansion knew how to repair it. Qingwen was already burning with a high fever, yet she forced herself to sit beneath the lamp and mend the cloak for Baoyu. Clenching her teeth, she stitched it together one careful stitch after another. By the time she finished, her clothes were soaked with sweat, her face had turned as pale as paper, and she nearly collapsed from exhaustion. Watching from beside her, Baoyu's heart ached. He repeatedly begged her to stop and rest. Qingwen only gave a cold smile. "Now you finally know how to care about someone? Where was that concern earlier?" Her words were harsh, but her hands never stopped sewing. That night, Baoyu sat quietly beside her, watching Qingwen beneath the flickering lamplight. Suddenly, he felt something he could not quite put into words. She did not seem like a servant girl； She was more like a living flame； But people like her were never destined to live long in the Jia Mansion. Later, the search of the Grand View Garden became the true turning point in Qingwen's fate. By then, chaos had already begun to spread throughout the Jia household. Lady Wang had grown suspicious that something improper was happening in the Happy Red Court. At the same time, Madam Wang Shanbao continually whispered in her ear, day after day, describing Qingwen as flirtatious, seductive, dressed like a fox spirit... and claiming that she was trying to entice Baoyu. Lady Wang had never liked Qingwen. The kind of girl she disliked most was one who was beautiful, intelligent, and proud. In her eyes, Qingwen was not merely a maid， She was a source of trouble. What made matters even worse was that Qingwen had originally come from Grandmother Jia's household. Deep down, Lady Wang understood perfectly well that Grandmother Jia favored Baoyu, and that Baoyu, in turn, cherished Qingwen. Driving Qingwen away was therefore more than simply punishing a servant. It was also a way of weakening Grandmother Jia's influence. From that moment on, everything followed naturally. By then, Qingwen was already gravely ill. She coughed constantly, her body burned with fever, and she could barely remain standing. Yet Lady Wang deliberately chose the very day when Qingwen was at her weakest to summon her. Whenever Baoyu later recalled that day, a chill still ran through his heart. Seated above her, Lady Wang looked coldly at the sick Qingwen. After asking only a few questions, she declared Qingwen to be "a vixen" and "a temptress." She even claimed that Qingwen suffered from "a woman's wasting disease" and that allowing her to remain in the Happy Red Court would endanger Baoyu's health. Qingwen tried again and again to defend herself，No one listened. At last, she suddenly understood，She had not lost because she had done something wrong. She had lost because she was too much like a real human being，She was too intelligent , too beautiful，too proud，and too unwilling to bow her head. Lady Wang immediately ordered her servants to drag Qingwen out of the mansion. That day, dressed in worn-out clothes, Qingwen was driven out of the Happy Red Court, she was so ill that she could scarcely walk. Baoyu was frantic with anxiety, But he did not dare stop them. For the first time, he truly understood that, even as the young master of the Jia family, he could not protect the person he most wanted to protect. After Qingwen was driven out, she was sent to the home of her cousin and his wife. It was a tiny, dilapidated place, with cold wind blowing through the broken walls. Lady Wang neither provided medicine nor arranged for anyone to care for her. Qingwen's illness quickly became far more serious. It was said that at night, when the pain became unbearable, she kept crying out, "Mother!" Yet no one came to her side. When Baoyu secretly went to see her, he could hardly recognize her. The Qingwen who had once been sharp-tongued, spirited, and full of life had become nothing more than skin and bones, lying on a broken bed, too weak even to speak. Baoyu sat beside her bed, his eyes instantly filled with tears.Qingwen, however, still forced herself to smile. "I always knew this day would come." At that moment, Baoyu felt as though a knife had been driven into his heart. He remembered Qingwen mending his Sparrow-Gold Fur Cloak.He remembered the carefree smile on her face when she tore the fan into pieces.He remembered the spirited way she stood with her hands on her hips, scolding the younger maids. Yet now, a girl who had once been so full of life was dying before his eyes, and there was nothing he could do. Before Qingwen died, she gave Baoyu the little red jacket she had worn close to her body. She also bit off several of her fingernails and left them for him. They were the last things she could leave behind, the final expression of her affection. Not long afterward, Qingwen died.When Baoyu heard the news, he sat there in stunned silence. He did not burst into tears. He did not lose control. He simply sat motionless for a very long time. For he suddenly realized that, in the Grand View Garden, those who were the purest, the truest, and the finest seemed never to live very long. Later, he secretly wrote "An Elegy for the Daughter of the Hibiscus." It was not merely a memorial essay ， it was, in truth, the first cry that Baoyu ever uttered against this world. He was mourning not only Qingwen, but all the young women who had been driven to their deaths. He was also mourning this great mansion, which appeared magnificent and prosperous, yet beneath its glitter quietly devoured one life after another. (To be continued)
