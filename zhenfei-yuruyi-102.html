But then she thought again: even if I had arrived earlier, could I have controlled the actions of the Red Guards? Who could have imagined that after so many hardships, after surviving so many disasters, she would, in the end—in the very end—die such a terrible death? No matter what, she was already ninety years old. She was a living piece of history, a harmless old woman who had never harmed anyone. Alas, Heaven above, why did this have to happen? Strictly speaking, Tan Yuxi and Consort Zhen were related by blood. Tan Yuxi’s ancestor had been Changshan, the Qing dynasty’s Guangzhou General. According to seniority in the family line, Tan should have called Consort Zhen her old grandaunt. Consort Zhen’s father was named Changxu, who rose to the position of Right Vice Minister of the Ministry of Revenue. Changshan was Changxu’s elder brother, which made him Consort Zhen’s uncle. Because her uncle Changshan served as Guangzhou General for as long as sixteen years, Consort Zhen had grown up from childhood in the Guangzhou General’s residence with him, only returning north to Beijing around the age of ten. Tan Yuxi’s ancestral surname, like Consort Zhen’s, was Tatara. After the Republic was founded, her clan changed the family name to Tan. When Beijing was peacefully liberated in 1949, Tan Yuxi was still a history student at Peking University. Influenced by the revolutionary enthusiasm of the time, she enrolled in North China University, a cadre training school run by the People’s Liberation Army—specifically by Lin Biao’s Fourth Field Army—to prepare personnel for taking over urban administration in areas formerly ruled by the Nationalists. In 1950, when the War to Resist U.S. Aggression and Aid Korea broke out, Tan Yuxi entered Korea with the army. She was struck by shrapnel from a bomb dropped by an American aircraft and was seriously wounded. After her injury, because of her physical condition, she transferred to the Beijing Municipal Public Security Bureau and became a female police officer. Because of her outstanding performance, together with her experience of combat in Korea, she became a key candidate for training and promotion. Before long, she was appointed chief of a police station. Tan Yuxi had majored in history at university, and had always been interested in the past. From the elders in her family, she had long known that among her ancestors and direct blood relatives there had once been a famous woman named Consort Zhen, a historical figure whose life had ended in tragedy. The hidden residence to which Consort Zhen returned in Beijing happened to fall within Tan Yuxi’s jurisdiction. When Tan learned that this elderly woman who had returned from overseas was the very Consort Zhen of legend—the one said to have been ordered by Empress Dowager Cixi to be thrown into a well and drowned, her own old grandaunt—the shock and excitement in her heart can easily be imagined. At the time, Consort Zhen was a key protected person specifically designated by the State Council. And the work of protecting Consort Zhen happened to fall under Tan Yuxi’s responsibility. For that reason, Tan poured all her energy into the task, never allowing any accident to occur, never permitting anything to disturb or interfere with Consort Zhen’s quiet life. She visited Consort Zhen regularly, learned what she needed, asked whether she had any difficulties, and did everything she could to help resolve them. It could be said that Tan Yuxi knew Consort Zhen’s situation in every detail. She often visited in the name of concern—and in truth, this was also part of her work—to talk and chat with Consort Zhen. Their conversations were always inseparable from Consort Zhen’s strange and extraordinary life story, and from the legendary tales of her years in overseas exile. Tan Yuxi loved history and literature. She had already begun preparing to write a book about Consort Zhen’s survival after a great disaster, her rescue, her exile overseas, and her secret return to China to live in hiding. It would reveal a historical mystery that had lasted for more than half a century. But who could have imagined it? Who could have imagined that she, already ninety years old, Consort Zhen who had survived calamity after calamity, would die today in this Cultural Revolution? Alas! Where is justice? Where is Heaven’s reason? Tan Yuxi carefully searched the scene and found several fragments of Consort Zhen’s jade ruyi. Consort Zhen had shown her this jade ruyi many times, so Tan was extremely familiar with this priceless treasure. She understood how Consort Zhen had devoted the strength of her entire life to preserving and protecting this jade ruyi of emerald-green jade. Even during the difficult years overseas, when life had no security and food and warmth were hard to come by, she had sold her other jewelry—but had preserved this jade ruyi alone. Because it was the token of love between her and her true love, Emperor Guangxu. Whenever she looked at it, the voice and smiling face of Emperor Guangxu from years long past would begin to appear before her eyes again. But who could have known that such a historically meaningful token, and at the same time such a rare treasure, would be destroyed so easily in this Great Proletarian Cultural Revolution? Tan Yuxi felt heartbreak and despair. Her heart bled inside. But what could she do? Even whether she could protect her own safety had become uncertain. In the past two days, people had already run to the police station shouting that they would smash the public security organs, the procuratorate, and the courts. Tan Yuxi truly could not understand it. What had happened to a perfectly good country? Truly, there were no words left but to question Heaven. (To be continued)
