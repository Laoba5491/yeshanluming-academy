This great literary masterpiece, Dream of the Red Chamber, has now existed for 266 years if counted from the Gengchen Manuscript, the final major revised edition produced during Cao Xueqin’s lifetime in the twenty-fifth year of the Qianlong Emperor, corresponding to the year 1760. For various reasons, Cao Xueqin completed only the first eighty chapters and was unable to finish the monumental work. Today, most scholars acknowledge that the final forty chapters were continued by Gao E on the basis of Cao Xueqin’s surviving drafts. In 1791, Gao E cooperated with Cheng Weiyuan to publish the 120-chapter “Cheng Jia Edition” of Dream of the Red Chamber, printed with movable wooden type, allowing the novel to circulate down to the present day. This is the 120-chapter version of Dream of the Red Chamber that we now read. From that publication until today, another 235 years have passed. For more than two centuries, this literary giant has stirred the entire Chinese literary world. Scholars of different schools of Redology have examined Cao Xueqin’s motives for writing the novel from every possible angle, studying and debating every aspect of the book. Entire schools of Redology have formed around it. Even today, arguments continue endlessly, and on many questions no consensus has ever been reached. So then, what exactly is Dream of the Red Chamber about? Seen from different perspectives and different points of view, everyone seems to see something different, like blind men feeling different parts of an elephant. Some see the wealth and splendor of the Jia family and regard the novel as a perfect window into the society of that era; some see the tragic love between Baoyu and Daiyu, believing it possesses an even deeper tragic power than Romeo and Juliet; some focus on The Song of All Good Things and the philosophical warnings it offers to the world; still others even study the exquisite cuisine of the Rongguo Mansion and create entire “Red Chamber culinary traditions” from it, with astonishing commercial success. It truly is a dazzling variety of interpretations. Each person takes from the novel what they desire, each finds what they seek. This alone shows how vast, broad, profound, and inexhaustible this masterpiece truly is. More than two hundred years have passed, and the study of it still continues. But I am different. I have read this book several times, and each time I read it, I feel a chill spread through my entire body. What I see is not the prosperous world of the Jia household, nor the tragic romance of Baoyu and Daiyu. What I see is a long black list of dead girls. And as I kept reading, I realized it was far more than that alone. That is why I added three extra words to the title: “and Others.” The black hole that was the Jia household devoured not only those pitiful girls, but many, many others as well ， including Wang Xifeng herself, the true power behind the Jia family. She possessed the greatest authority, the sharpest schemes, and was always the strong one, the victor. She was not merely a victim, but often a perpetrator of harm against others. And yet even she could not escape being swallowed by that black hole. In the end she met the most miserable fate of all: cast aside by her husband Jia Lian, crushed by poverty and illness, and after death denied even a proper coffin. Her body was wrapped only in a tattered mat and abandoned in a paupers’ graveyard. And in truth, was she not also one more soul consumed by that same black hole…? Behind the dazzling splendor and blooming prosperity of the Rongguo Mansion stood row after row of girls walking steadily toward death. Within the Grand View Garden, amid flowers, laughter, songs, and beauty, everyone appeared so radiant and enviable. Yet Cao Xueqin had already foretold the truth long ago, warning his readers: “One thousand crimson flowers weep as one, Ten thousand beauties share the same sorrow.” Behind all that brilliance and luxury stood one flower-like young girl after another, swallowed whole by the black hole of the Jia household. One after another, girls whose lives were devoured. Some died for love, some for chastity and reputation, some for power, some for feudal morality, some for the darkness of the human heart, and some because they lived in a world that could not tolerate genuine feeling at all… And most terrifying of all: these deaths were rarely grand or dramatic. Sometimes all it took was a single sentence, a well, a wall, an illness, a marriage arrangement, an insult, a beating, an expulsion， or merely a little despair. And then the person was gone. The young Jia Baoyu grew up surrounded by powder and perfume, encircled by innocent girls. He loved all of them, stayed close to them, cherished them, and even wished to protect them. He believed that girls were made of water ， pure and clear like mountain spring water itself. Yet in the real world, he was powerless to do anything for them, much less protect them. All he could do was watch helplessly as they walked one by one toward death. The only thing left to him afterward was to secretly mourn their spirits, shed a few bitter tears, and wait for the next identical tragedy to happen again. He watched helplessly as those girls， the most beautiful, most vivid, most innocent, most spiritual souls ， died one after another. He could not stop it. He could not save them. Such suffering is almost beyond human endurance. Each death became another black dagger stabbing into Baoyu’s heart. In the end, he became utterly disappointed in this world, utterly hopeless. Unable to change anything, he finally chose to leave… Therefore, what Cao Xueqin truly wrote in Dream of the Red Chamber may not merely be a love story. Rather, it is a vast lament for how beauty itself is destroyed. （To be continued）
